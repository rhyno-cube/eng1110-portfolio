MWA 1: Memoir

The Power I Struggled to Concede

For most of my life, I believed that being sensitive was a weakness. I remember sitting in my room one evening after a long day and mentally reliving conversations. Someone had made a careless comment, and although they probably forgot about it minutes later, I continued to think about it for hours. My thoughts were loud, but the only sound in the room was the soft hum of the ceiling fan. I thought about what I had heard before: You're too personal about things. You're too emotional. You must have thicker skin.

I discovered somewhere along the line that strength meant not being impacted. It required maintaining composure and avoiding overreaction or emotion. I therefore made an effort to become that version of myself. When something disturbed me, I stopped talking. Even when it hurt, I laughed it off. I told myself that life would be easier if I could just get tougher.

But suppressing emotions doesn't make them disappear. It merely buried them.

The turning point came when I began working closely with children. At first, I didn't think much about it. Help, guidance, and listening were all that was needed. But as time passed, I realized something. The children turned to me when they were distressed or feeling overburdened. They would either sit next to me in silence or discuss topics that were very important to them. They confided in me about their feelings.

On the verge of tears, a child was upset about a trivial matter one afternoon. While others brushed it off as "not a big deal," I knelt down and listened. I could see the embarrassment on their faces as their hands clenched. Instead of telling them to calm down, I told them that it was okay to feel upset. I stayed there until their breathing slowed and their shoulders relaxed.

I had never allowed myself to see the very trait I had tried before, but in that moment, I realized that it was the very quality I had tried to hide that was responsible for my high level of interpersonal connection. I wasn't weak because I was sensitive. It was consciousness. It was compassion. It made it possible for me to see things that others might have missed, like a change in tone, a person avoiding eye contact, or a silence that conveyed more meaning than words. What I used to consider a weakness turned out to be a strength.

In retrospect, I realize that being strong does not equate to being emotionless. It entails choosing how to react and having emotional awareness. It entails enduring discomfort while sitting rather than escaping it. I still have strong emotions. I still occasionally re-record conversations. Now, however, I view it differently, rather than judging myself for it. There's nothing I need to do about sensitivity. I've mastered the art of carrying it with assurance.

I spent years trying to become someone I wasn't. I now realize that the strength I was looking for was always there, concealed within the very softness I had previously attempted to eradicate.
